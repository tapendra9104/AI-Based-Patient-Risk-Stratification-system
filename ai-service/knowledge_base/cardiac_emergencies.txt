CARDIAC EMERGENCY ASSESSMENT AND MANAGEMENT
=============================================

ACUTE CORONARY SYNDROME (ACS):
- Unstable Angina: Chest pain at rest or with minimal exertion. No troponin elevation. ECG may show ST depression or T-wave inversion.
- NSTEMI (Non-ST Elevation MI): Elevated troponin with chest pain. No ST elevation on ECG. Requires antiplatelet therapy, anticoagulation, and early invasive strategy.
- STEMI (ST Elevation MI): ST elevation on ECG with chest pain. Requires emergent reperfusion therapy (PCI within 90 minutes or fibrinolysis within 30 minutes of first medical contact).

CHEST PAIN — RED FLAGS:
- Pain radiating to left arm, jaw, or back
- Associated with diaphoresis (sweating), nausea, or dyspnea
- Worse with exertion, relieved by rest or nitroglycerin
- New onset in patient with multiple cardiovascular risk factors
- Sudden tearing pain radiating to the back (consider aortic dissection)

HEART FAILURE CLASSIFICATION (NYHA):
- Class I: No symptoms with ordinary physical activity
- Class II: Mild symptoms (fatigue, mild dyspnea) with ordinary activity
- Class III: Marked limitation of physical activity. Comfortable at rest.
- Class IV: Symptoms at rest. Any physical activity causes discomfort.

HEART FAILURE SIGNS AND SYMPTOMS:
- Dyspnea on exertion progressing to orthopnea and paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea
- Peripheral edema (ankle swelling)
- Jugular venous distension
- Pulmonary crackles (rales)
- S3 gallop on cardiac auscultation
- Weight gain from fluid retention
- Reduced exercise tolerance and fatigue

ARRHYTHMIA ASSESSMENT:
- Atrial Fibrillation: Irregular irregular rhythm. Risk of stroke. CHA2DS2-VASc score determines anticoagulation need.
- Ventricular Tachycardia: Wide complex tachycardia. Can be hemodynamically unstable. May require cardioversion.
- Bradycardia: Heart rate < 60 bpm. Symptomatic bradycardia requires atropine or temporary pacing.
- Supraventricular Tachycardia: Heart rate 150-220 bpm. Regular narrow complex. Vagal maneuvers or adenosine may terminate.

BLOOD PRESSURE CRISES:
- Hypertensive Urgency: BP > 180/120 mmHg WITHOUT end-organ damage. Gradual reduction over 24-48 hours with oral medications.
- Hypertensive Emergency: BP > 180/120 mmHg WITH end-organ damage (encephalopathy, retinal hemorrhage, acute kidney injury, aortic dissection). Requires IV medications in ICU setting with 25% BP reduction in first hour.

CARDIAC BIOMARKERS AND INTERPRETATION:
- Troponin I/T: Gold standard for myocardial injury. Rises 3-6 hours after onset, peaks 12-24 hours. Serial measurements at 0, 3, and 6 hours.
- BNP/NT-proBNP: Elevated in heart failure. BNP > 100 pg/mL or NT-proBNP > 300 pg/mL suggests heart failure.
- D-dimer: Elevated in pulmonary embolism, deep vein thrombosis, aortic dissection. High sensitivity but low specificity.
- CK-MB: Less specific than troponin. May be useful for detecting reinfarction.

STROKE ASSESSMENT (BE-FAST):
- Balance: Sudden loss of balance or coordination
- Eyes: Sudden vision changes in one or both eyes
- Face: Facial drooping on one side
- Arms: Arm weakness or numbness (drift test)
- Speech: Slurred speech or difficulty understanding
- Time: Time to call emergency services (every minute counts)
- Ischemic stroke: Thrombolysis (tPA) within 4.5 hours of symptom onset. Thrombectomy up to 24 hours in selected patients.
- Hemorrhagic stroke: Blood pressure control, reversal of anticoagulation, neurosurgical consultation.
